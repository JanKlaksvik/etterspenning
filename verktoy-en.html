Grouting equipment

Grouting is often where quality is won or lost. Focus on repeatable mixing, parameter control and proper cleaning/maintenance.

  • Before grouting starts, perform a 3-hour test, spread plate and cup test, plus density/weight check.
  • Clean machine and hose at least every 4 hours.
  • Use a routine for measuring water volume, mixing time, temperature and consistency.
  • Clean immediately after use - cement residue damages valves and hoses.

Stressing jack

The jack is the central force source in stressing work. What matters is not only nominal capacity, but also that applied force can be kept stable so measurement and documentation remain reliable over time.

  • Check capacity, stroke length, wedges (wear/grip), and that the jack runs smoothly without jerks.
  • Check for leaks at seals and connections before use.
  • Verify that the master wedges in the jack are not damaged or dirty.
  • Log serial number and linkage to project/protocol.
  • Verify calibration certificate, max 12 months old.

Pump (hydraulic power pack)

The pump must deliver stable pressure and flow. The most common field failures come from hoses/couplings, oil contamination or incorrect pressure-relief handling.

  • Check oil level and confirm pressure relief is working.
  • Check that the pressure gauge is readable and physically protected.
  • Let the pump warm up before stressing in cold weather.
  • Verify correct connection on both jack and pump - wrong coupling can be life-threatening.

Hoses, couplings and safety equipment

Hoses are often a weak link. A hose rupture under pressure is a serious incident.

  • Visual inspection: cracks, bulges, kinks, abrasion against reinforcement/concrete edges.
  • Check locking ring/clips on quick couplings - always use correct safety retention.
  • Use hose protection where there is risk of impact or splash.
  • Keep absorbent mats available where leakage risk exists.
  • Keep personnel out of the line of hoses and jacks under pressure.

Force measurement: pressure gauge vs. load cell

Traditionally, force is calculated from pressure (gauge) and a calibrated area ratio. A more robust solution is a load cell that measures force directly.

  • Pressure gauge: requires stable calibration of jack/pump/gauge as one system.
  • Load cell: provides direct force measurement and can reduce cross-equipment calibration dependency.
  • Regardless of method: traceable calibration and a deviation-handling routine are critical.
  • Maximum 12 months since last calibration, and total system deviation should remain within project acceptance limits.

Measurement instrument for stressing

Accurate elongation measurement is critical and often decisive for whether an anchor is accepted or rejected.

  • The instrument should be protected against weather and mechanical impact (rain, wind, shocks).
  • Calibration with traceability is essential for verifiable documentation.
  • Not all digital instruments tolerate rain or low temperatures - verify IP rating and operating temperature.
